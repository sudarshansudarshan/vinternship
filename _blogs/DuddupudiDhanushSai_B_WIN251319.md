title: "Becoming Comfortable with Being Uncomfortable"
author: Dhanush Sai

When I first joined the Vinternship at VLED Lab, I thought I knew what learning looked like.

Attend sessions. Take notes. Complete assignments. Move forward.

But very quickly, I realized this was different.

There were no shortcuts. No spoon-fed solutions. No one constantly reminding us to stay on track. Instead, there was space — space to struggle, space to think, and space to figure things out on our own.

And honestly, that was uncomfortable.

In the beginning, learning through ViBe felt intense. It demanded consistency. If I skipped even a day, it showed. If I didn’t practice, I felt stuck. The MERN stack was not just theory; it required action. Writing code daily wasn’t optional — it was survival.

There were moments I doubted myself.

When case studies began, things became even more real. It wasn’t about whether I remembered syntax. It was about whether I could think like a developer. Breaking down problems, structuring logic, debugging patiently — these were skills no textbook could fully teach.

I remember staring at errors for long minutes, sometimes hours. At first, I saw errors as proof that I wasn’t good enough. Slowly, I started seeing them as direction. Every bug pointed somewhere. Every failure revealed a gap in understanding.

That shift changed everything.

Consistency became more powerful than motivation. There were days I didn’t feel productive, but I showed up anyway. And that mattered more than any burst of enthusiasm.

Breakout room discussions were another quiet turning point. Listening to others explain their thought process made me realize there isn’t just one way to solve a problem. Sometimes, someone else’s simple idea would unlock something complex in my mind. Learning stopped being individual — it became collaborative.

Preparing for the viva pushed me to reflect deeply. It wasn’t about memorizing answers. It was about understanding why I did what I did in my code. Explaining logic out loud made me aware of how much I had actually grown.

And growth didn’t feel dramatic.

It felt subtle.

I noticed I wasn’t afraid of complex tasks anymore. I didn’t panic when something didn’t work. I started trusting the process — that if I kept showing up and kept thinking, I would eventually figure it out.

This internship didn’t just teach me MERN stack development.

    It taught me patience.
    It taught me discipline.
    It taught me how to sit with confusion without quitting.
    It taught me that real learning happens when nobody is forcing you — when it’s just you and your commitment.

If someone asked me what stayed with me after the sessions ended, I wouldn’t talk about frameworks or databases.

I would talk about resilience.

Because somewhere between the first line of code and the last case study, I became comfortable with being uncomfortable.

And I think that is where real growth begins.
